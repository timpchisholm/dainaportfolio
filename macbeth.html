Macbeth was my first foray into costumes for Shakespeare at Play, and really paved the way for the continuing aesthetic of shows to come. The director, Tim Chisholm, had a very clear idea of what he wanted: lots of knits and cargo pants/vaguely militaristic looks in neutral colours. I chose mostly dark blues, forest greens and neutral tones from brown to black, across the board. It was important to have different looks for Macbeth and Lady Macbeth as they evolve throughout the story from nobles to royalty, to royalty that descend into madness. This kept the overall tone of this show moody and dark.

The Witches

I was inspired by the Skeksis and the Mystics in Jim Henson’s The Dark Crystal, as far as the look goes: floor length robes of some kind with a heaviness to them. One of the witches brought in this beautiful moss green crushed velvet curtain material. The end result came from all of us playing in rehearsal with this fabric, which was the cornerstone of the characterization of the witches and set the tone for the aesthetic of the whole piece.

Lady Macbeth

I put Lady M in knits in the beginning. When she becomes the queen, I put her in a lighter knit with shorter sleeves and sequins to make her a bit more glamorous. It was the actress’s idea to wear the tule skirt, which I thought was a great choice to hint at her underlying mental fragility. It also was such a feminine look for a play populated with men, and I really liked that softer, more feminine contrast with the knits and cargos the men wore.

Macbeth

Macbeth’s looks evolved throughout the story, beginning with the heavy knit sweater, military shirt and grey pants. The sweater was meant to represent his “armour,” which is why he wears it again in the final scene as he fights with Macduff. When he becomes the king, I put him in a a clean white shirt, tailored black coat, and we smoothed out his hair to give him an air of royalty and cleanliness as his character does blacker and blacker things.

'The Soldiers'

The soldiers wore sweaters and scarves to denote the chilly, desolate atmosphere of the Scottish moors. I kept them all in earth tones all around. Malcolm dressed a little bit more “preppy” and less militaristic to show the fact that he is a prince, and I had Banquo in a beautiful green sweater I found at a military surplus store. I wanted Banquo to look like a soldier the most.
