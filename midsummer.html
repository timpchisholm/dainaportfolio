Dream was the third production I worked on as a costume designer for Shakespeare at Play with director Tim Chisholm. A Midsummer Night’s Dream was the first comedy we did, with a very different tone than Macbeth or Hamlet, so Tim wanted the look of the show to be quite distinct. He wanted to play more with style and design, while keeping the minimal, clean Shakespeare at Play aesthetic in tact. I was excited to play with brighter colours, and had several ideas for the fairies and various props I had to make until I landed on the final result. What I loved about Dream from a costume designer’s perspective was that there are three distinct “worlds” the characters all come from: the lovers from the court of Athens, the fairies from the supernatural forest, and the mechanicals from the working class of Athens. I had to think of ways to create differences between the three, while keeping some sense of unity to the whole, but also giving each character a look that spoke about who they are.

The Fairies

The director wanted to have a visual representation of the fact that the faires are creatures of the earth, with an air of mischievousness and mystery. I put them all in black dance wear and painted their clothing with patterns that resemble the veins of leaves. I differentiated the two “camps” of fairies by using different coloured paint and accessories: silver for Titania’s fairies (to represent the moon, women and fertility), and gold for Oberon’s fairies (to represent the sun, masculinity and virility). It was a subtle difference, but I think, effective.

The 'Rude Mechanicals'

For their basic, everyday looks, I wanted them to look very different from one another because their personalities are so unique, and each of them has a different trade that I wanted to represent. To keep them cohesive, I put them in warm earth tones with some “blue collar” textures and fabrics like cotton, denim and corduroy. For their "costumes", there were bits and pieces I handmade, with the idea that these items were to be made “badly,” as if these working-class men had made them themselves.

Theseus' Court

The court was a challenge because I needed to find a way to represent Athens. I put them in various neutral tones in the first scene, with some subtle colour choices: pale pink, black and white, greys, and dark blue, then decided that to have them all in white tones at the end for the wedding. The lovers in the forest was where I punched the colours up to show that these people don’t come from the world of the forest. They needed to be very visible because they unintentionally become the pantomime the fairies amuse themselves with. Made all the more obvious by the fact that the fairies’ costumes blend into their surroundings a bit more.
